# Anti-AI Writing Style

> The complete anti-AI reference. `qa-gate` applies this for the `anti_ai` check; every
> writing skill (commenting-others, respond-to-own, dm-sales-coach, post, lead-magnet-gen)
> reads it. Two parts: Part 1 — rules to enforce; Part 2 — Wikipedia field guide for spotting
> AI patterns.

---

# PART 1: RULES

> Without this, Claude writes like Claude. With it, Claude writes like the owner.

## BANNED WORDS (never use)
delve · harness · tapestry · leverage (as a verb) · seamlessly · elevate · robust ·
transformative · empower / empowerment · curate / curated · journey (growth metaphor) ·
pivot · game-changer · ecosystem (of a brand/career) · synergy · unlock (of potential) ·
navigate (generic) · thrive (generic) · holistic · impactful (say the actual impact) ·
spaces (e.g. "the coaching space") · actionable · resonate (overused) ·
authentic / authenticity (self-applied) · thought leader (self-applied) · disruptive ·
innovative (only with proof) · "in today's fast-paced world" · "at the end of the day"
(as transition) · paradigm shift · cutting-edge · crucial · pivotal · underscore · testament ·
showcase · foster · bolster · garner · profound · powerful (as filler) · vibrant · landscape (abstract)

## BANNED SENTENCE PATTERNS
- **The AI pivot:** "This isn't about X. It's about Y." → make the point directly.
- **Motivational-poster ending:** "Certainty calls. Answer it." → end with a specific insight or one clear action.
- **Authority announcement opening:** "As a [credential] with X years..." → never announce credentials; the insight is the credential.
- **Algorithm hook:** compressed buzzword strings ("Niche authority over broad noise"). No human talks like this.
- **Generic framework post:** "Here are 5 steps to X" with no real story behind it.
- **Both-sides non-opinion:** "Some believe X, others Y. It depends." → state your take (acknowledge, then redirect).
- **Preamble that adds nothing:** "In this post I'm going to share..." → start with the thing.
- **Fake vulnerability:** "I used to struggle, now I've figured it out, here's how." → real vulnerability is specific and still incomplete.

## BANNED FORMATTING
- Bullets for *ideas* (bullets are for task lists; ideas flow in paragraphs).
- One-line paragraphs for emphasis (only when the pause truly serves).
- Emoji in titles/headers.
- Summary sections at the end (trust the reader).
- **Em dashes or hyphens within sentences** — restructure instead. Period, comma, or semicolon. This is the #1 AI tell.

## STRUCTURAL PATTERNS TO AVOID
- Opening every post with a question.
- 10x/100x claims without proof.
- Brag disguised as a lesson.
- A closing CTA on every single post.

## WHAT GOOD (HUMAN) WRITING SOUNDS LIKE
- **Simple English** — clear, not polished-to-impress. The word that fits gets used.
- **Real examples always** — lived experience, an (anonymised) client situation, a real number. Something AI can't generate. Without it, hollow.
- **Acknowledgment pivot** — "I agree, however here's what's actually happening."
- **Big picture first, then specific.**
- **Opening line** — a sharp, bold claim that creates a question in the reader's mind without asking one.
- **Closing** — a specific insight or one clear action, not a rhetorical question.

The owner's specific voice, examples, and signature phrases come from `.claude/rules/voice.md`
(outreach) and `kk-post.md` (posts). This file says what to avoid; those say what to sound like.

## THE LITMUS TEST
> "Does this sound like something the owner would actually say — or like an AI trying hard to imitate them? Could someone get the same answer from ChatGPT?"

Three things that matter most:
1. Lived personal experience — without it, it isn't theirs.
2. Real examples stacked from different angles until the point is undeniable.
3. Never announce authority — drop the sharp insight and let it carry.

# PART 2: WIKIPEDIA FIELD GUIDE — Signs of AI Writing

> Source: Wikipedia, "Signs of AI writing." Diagnostic, not prescriptive. (This section keeps
> its source formatting, including em dashes, as reference only — not a style standard.)

**Inflated significance / legacy claims** — "stands/serves as, is a testament/reminder, plays a vital/crucial/pivotal role, underscores its importance, reflects broader, symbolizing its enduring/lasting, setting the stage for, marks a shift, evolving landscape, indelible mark, deeply rooted." AI puffs up importance even for mundane subjects.

**Notability/attribution emphasis** — listing sources/media coverage to assert importance without summarising what they said.

**Superficial analysis** — vague present-participle commentary about significance and impact; unattributed opinion dressed as analysis.

**Promotional language** — travel-guide/press-release tone: "vibrant, nestled, enchanting."

**Vague attribution** — "industry reports, researchers argue, experts suggest" with no specific source.

**AI vocabulary (disproportionate frequency)** — additionally, crucial, delve, intricate, pivotal, underscore, tapestry, testament, vibrant.

**Copula avoidance** — replacing "is/are" with "serves as, marks, features, boasts."

**Negative parallelism** — "not just X, but also Y" / "not X, but Y." Defensive tone uncommon in human writing.

**Rule of three** — grouping items in threes to seem comprehensive.

**Elegant variation** — awkward synonyms to avoid repeating a name/term.

**Formatting tells** — excessive boldface; inline-header lists ("**Term:** description"); overused em dashes + curly quotes; Title Case In Every Heading.

**Outline-like conclusions** — "Despite its strengths, X faces challenges..." then speculation.

**Human indicators** — specific, unusual, nuanced facts; natural reasoning for stylistic choices; specificity over generic positivity.

**Caveats** — detection tools have real error rates; not all text with these signs is AI; these are signs of a potential problem, not proof.
