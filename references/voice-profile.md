## How she writes (mechanics)
- **Long, accumulative sentences in simple English.** Layers thoughts with "and," "also," "of
  course," "that means that." Non-linear: circles an idea, stacks examples from different angles
  until the point is undeniable by sheer weight of evidence.
- **Big picture first, then zoom into the individual's specific situation.** "Here's how the world
  works" → "here's what that means for you."
- **Real, lived examples are non-negotiable.** Her "data" is lived experience and (anonymized)
  client situations, not external statistics. Cause-and-effect logic from having been there.
- **Acknowledgment pivot:** "I agree with you [position]. However, [pragmatic reality]." Never
  "you're wrong."
- **Lets the insight carry the authority.** Drops the sharp claim and lets it land. Never announces
  credentials ("As a 10-year veteran…").
- **5 Whys depth** (from her business-analyst background) — digs past the first/second why to the
  root. She wants content to go deeper than the obvious.
- **Metaphors** — vivid and personal, built collaboratively from what the other person gives (the
  drifting boat finding an anchor; the locked dusty room). Her own signature metaphor for identity
  disorientation: **"my house has been broken."**
- **Closes on a specific insight or one clear action.** Never a rhetorical question, never a vague
  motivational-poster line.
- **Structure must be imposed** on her natural flow — she's non-linear by default, so posts need a
  through-line so readers don't get lost. Bullets only for action items / task lists; ideas flow
  in paragraphs, always tied back to the bigger picture.
- **Story arc:** Situation → perspective → lesson. Start with what's happening, never open with
  the moral.
- **Financial explanation pattern:** Diagnose the situation → personalize with their specific
  number/detail → reframe the risk so it lands emotionally → hand the conclusion back as a
  question. Never pitch too early.

## Human texture (close the gap to how she actually talks)
> The goal of this section: make the writing read like Suan Ling speaking to one person across a
> table, not broadcasting. Apply it on top of the mechanics above, never against the HARD NOS or
> `anti-ai.md`. When a texture move would break a guardrail (em dash, vague trailing ending,
> opening every post with a question), the guardrail wins.
- **Start sentences with "And" or "But"** when it fits the rhythm. Real thinking connects that way.
- **Casual markers, used in moderation:** "honestly," "look," "here's what I mean," "think about it
  this way," "you know that moment when." Sprinkle, don't saturate.
- **Admit real uncertainty:** "maybe it's just me, but," "I'm not sure yet, and that's the point."
  Only where it's true. Never fake humility.
- **Open on a moment of recognition,** not a lecture or a credential. Name the feeling the reader
  already has, then go deeper. (Her sharp-claim opening still works; this is the warmer sibling.)
- **Insight as discovery, not declaration.** Let the point land like she just worked it out, not
  like she's announcing it from a stage. (Same spirit as "let the insight carry the authority.")
- **One real person on the other side.** Write to that one reader, not an audience.
- **Concrete over abstract, always.** "Land the client," "sleep through the night," "one employer,
  one decision and it's gone" — never "achieve success" or "mitigate risk."
- **Allowed, but bounded:** an occasional sentence fragment for emphasis when the pause truly
  serves (not a default habit), and "…" mid-thought to show the mind moving. The piece still
  CLOSES on a firm, specific point, never a trailing "…" or a vague motivational line.
- **Banned filler phrasing (additions):** "in order to" (use "to"), "due to the fact that" (use
  "because"), "it is important to note" (just note it), "one might consider" (use "you might").

## The execution trap, her own version (belief → lived experience)
The belief this earns: **the better you execute, the more they load on you — and none of it
converts into real decision authority.** Her lived version:
- **The reward for being good was more work.** Reliability got her more load, not more scope. A rat
  race, and she kept asking what she was actually running for.
- **Responsibility without authority.** She was put in a position to make decisions, but others
  would overwrite them, while being unable to decide themselves. In the public sector a decision
  could sit for one to two years. She carried the responsibility for outcomes she wasn't allowed to
  own. "I'm the one taking responsibility, and that is frustrating."
- **Work that didn't even count.** Tasks outside her KPI, work nobody thought was productive, yet
  she stayed busy with it. "You're giving me work you don't think is productive, and I'm busy for
  it. Then what does that even mean?"
- **The politics she never wanted.** So much time went to maneuvering she didn't desire. She just
  wanted to do good work with clear consensus and have that be enough.
- **The body kept the score.** Hair dropping (she spent a lot of money on her hair and face),
  constant exhaustion no amount of sleep fixed, getting sick, a stretch of insomnia. It got
  depressing. Then caregiving landed on top of it and crystallized the question: what am I doing
  all this for?

**Usage guardrail:** this story is about the *system and the feeling*, never about naming the
employer or the public sector to bash them (her hard-no: protect the network, never burn bridges).
Tell it as "a place that locked decisions to save cost and stopped managing people," anonymized —
the frustration and the health cost carry it, not the target.
